# Colonial Period in Tamil Nadu: European Powers and British Rule

## How did European powers first arrive in Tamil Nadu?

European contact with Tamil Nadu began with the Portuguese in the early 16th century and escalated through Dutch, Danish, French, and British involvement, culminating in British colonial rule that lasted until 1947.

**Portuguese (1498–17th century):**
Vasco da Gama's arrival in Calicut (Kerala) in 1498 initiated Portuguese engagement with South India. The Portuguese established a trading presence along the Tamil Nadu coast:
- **Tuticorin (Thoothukudi):** A major Portuguese trading post for the pearl fishery trade. The Parava fishing community's mass conversion to Christianity under Francis Xavier (1544–45 CE) — creating the first large-scale Christian community in South India — was organized partly through Portuguese naval protection against Arab competitors in the pearl trade.
- **Nagapattinam:** A Portuguese fort and trading station on the Coromandel coast, later transferred to the Dutch.
- **St. Thomas Mount (Chennai):** Portuguese tradition holds that the apostle Thomas was martyred here in 72 CE. The Portuguese built a church on St. Thomas Mount in 1523 CE over what they claimed was the original martyr site.

**Dutch (1600–18th century):**
The Dutch East India Company (VOC) replaced the Portuguese as the dominant European power on the Coromandel coast by the mid-17th century:
- **Pulicat (Pazhaverkadu):** North of Chennai, Pulicat was the VOC's headquarters on the Coromandel coast from 1613 to 1690 CE. The Dutch Pulicat factory was a major trading center for textiles — particularly the kalamkari and chintz cotton fabrics that were among the most sought-after commodities in European markets.
- **Nagapattinam:** Captured from the Portuguese in 1658 CE, Nagapattinam became the major Dutch port in Tamil Nadu until its capture by the British in 1781.
- **Impact on local production:** Dutch demand for Indian textiles significantly shaped the weaving economy of the Coromandel coast, creating specialized production centers for export-oriented textile manufacturing.

**Danish (1620–1845):**
The Danish East India Company established a colony at Tranquebar (Tharangambadi) on the Coromandel coast in 1620 CE:
- Tranquebar remained a Danish colony until 1845 CE when it was sold to the British.
- The Dansborg Fort (1620) still stands.
- The Danish missionary Bartholomäus Ziegenbalg arrived at Tranquebar in 1706 CE — he learned Tamil, created a Tamil printing press (the first in India), translated the New Testament into Tamil (1714 CE), and compiled the first Tamil-German dictionary. Ziegenbalg's Tamil scholarship laid foundations for modern Tamil linguistics.

**French (1674–1954):**
The French East India Company (Compagnie des Indes Orientales) established Pondicherry (Puducherry) as their major South Indian base:
- Founded as a French trading post in 1674 CE on land purchased from the local Bijapur Sultanate governor.
- Pondicherry became the capital of French India (Établissements français dans l'Inde).
- **Joseph François Dupleix (Governor 1742–1754):** The most ambitious French administrator in India, Dupleix pursued a policy of political intervention in Indian succession disputes — supporting local rulers militarily in exchange for territorial concessions. His strategy nearly made France the dominant European power in India but was ultimately defeated by Robert Clive and the British.
- **Battle of Wandiwash (1760):** The decisive engagement of the Third Carnatic War, where British forces under Sir Eyre Coote defeated the French under the Marquis de Lally. Pondicherry was captured in 1761 CE and returned to France in 1763 under the Treaty of Paris.
- Pondicherry remained French until 1954 CE (legally merged with India in 1962), creating a distinctive Franco-Tamil culture visible in the town's architecture, cuisine, and administrative heritage.

## What was the rise of British power in Tamil Nadu?

**East India Company and the Carnatic Wars:**
The British East India Company established its first major South Indian base at Madraspattinam (Chennai/Madras) in 1639–40 CE, through a grant from the local Vijayanagara Nayak official. Fort St. George was constructed and became the nucleus of the city of Madras.

The mid-18th century saw the three Carnatic Wars (1744–1763) between British and French for dominance over the Carnatic (the Tamil and Telugu coastal region):

**First Carnatic War (1744–1748):** Inconclusive; fought as a side-theater of the War of Austrian Succession.

**Second Carnatic War (1749–1754):** Robert Clive (then a young Company employee) distinguished himself at the Siege of Arcot (1751), holding the Nawab of Arcot's capital against a large force — a turning point that established British military prestige in South India.

**Third Carnatic War (1757–1763):** British victory at Wandiwash (1760) ended French power in India.

**The Nawabs of Arcot:**
Following the decline of Mughal authority, the Carnatic (Tamil Nadu and Andhra) was nominally governed by Nawabs (governors) from Arcot, subordinate to the Nizam of Hyderabad. The Nawabs of Arcot became increasingly dependent on the British East India Company for military support, incurring enormous debts. By the late 18th century, the Nawabs were effectively British puppets, and the Carnatic was formally absorbed into British India in 1801 CE.

## Who were the major resistors to British rule in Tamil Nadu?

**The Polygar Wars (Paalaiyakkarar Wars, 1799–1805 CE):**
The British attempt to abolish the Paalaiyakkarar (Poligar) system — the network of armed chieftains established by the Nayaks — led to major armed resistance:

**Veerapandiya Kattabomman (1760–1799):** The Poligar of Panchalankurichi in Tirunelveli district who refused to pay tribute to the British East India Company and resisted British authority militarily. After a period of successful guerrilla resistance, he was captured, tried, and publicly hanged at Kayathar in 1799 CE. Kattabomman is celebrated in Tamil Nadu as one of the first freedom fighters against British colonialism.

**Maruthu Brothers (Vella Maruthu and Chinna Maruthu):** Chieftains of Sivaganga who continued resistance after Kattabomman's death, coordinating a broad coalition of polygar resistance. They issued what is sometimes called the "first proclamation of independence" from Tiruppathur in 1801 — calling on the people to expel the British. Captured and executed in 1801 CE.

**Dheeran Chinnamalai:** The Poligar of Kongu Nadu (Coimbatore region) who fought the British in alliance with the Mysore forces of Tipu Sultan and later independently. He was captured and hanged in 1805 CE.

**Velu Nachiyar (1730–1796):** The queen of Sivaganga who fought the British in the 1780s, making her one of the earliest women freedom fighters against British colonialism. She allied with Hyder Ali of Mysore and the Maratha powers. She is notable for creating what some historians describe as an early form of suicide bomb attack — Kuyili, a woman soldier, is said to have immolated herself while carrying ammunition into a British arsenal.

## What was the social and economic impact of British colonial rule on Tamil Nadu?

**Land revenue and economic disruption:**
The British Ryotwari system (introduced by Thomas Munro in 1820) — a system of direct land settlement between the state and individual cultivators — transformed Tamil Nadu's agricultural economy. While eliminating some intermediaries, it also imposed cash-based taxation that created vulnerability to crop failure and drove peasants into debt.

**The indigo and opium trade:**
Tamil Nadu's agriculture was significantly disrupted by British demands for cash crops — particularly cotton for the Lancashire textile mills and indigo. The transition from food crops to cash crops contributed to famines.

**Madras Famine of 1876–78:**
One of the deadliest famines in Indian history, the Great Famine of 1876–78 affected Tamil Nadu (then Madras Presidency) severely. An estimated 5.5 million people died across South India. The British colonial government's response — continuing grain exports from famine-affected areas and refusing to interfere with market prices — was condemned at the time by critics including William Digby and has been characterized by later historians as a policy failure of catastrophic proportions.

**Caste, education, and social reform:**
British colonial rule had complex effects on Tamil social structures:
- Christian missionary education (particularly by the London Missionary Society and the American Madura Mission) introduced English-medium education, which created new social mobility opportunities but initially primarily benefited upper-caste communities.
- The census operations conducted by British administrators hardened and formalized caste identities in ways that had lasting social consequences.
- At the same time, colonial-era social reform movements — the Self-Respect Movement of Periyar (E. V. Ramasamy, 1925 onward) specifically challenged caste hierarchy and Brahminical dominance.
