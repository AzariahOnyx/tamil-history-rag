# Vijayanagara Empire and the Nayak Period in Tamil Nadu

## How did the Vijayanagara Empire relate to Tamil Nadu?

The Vijayanagara Empire (1336–1646 CE), founded by the brothers Harihara and Bukka Raya in the Deccan, became the dominant power of peninsular India for three centuries and had profound consequences for Tamil Nadu. Following the devastation of the Delhi Sultanate invasions and the collapse of the Chola and later Hoysala and Pandya powers, Vijayanagara emerged as the great defender of Hindu civilization against the northward pressure of the Sultanates.

**Vijayanagara control over Tamil Nadu:**
The empire incorporated Tamil Nadu progressively from the mid-14th century onward. Tamil Nadu was administered through a system of governors called Nayaks (Telugu: Naayakudu, Tamil: Naayakkar) — military commanders appointed by the Vijayanagara emperor to govern specific territories.

**The Krishnadevaraya connection:**
Krishnadevaraya (reigned 1509–1529 CE), the greatest Vijayanagara emperor, patronized Telugu literature (he himself was a distinguished Telugu poet — his Amuktamalyada is a Telugu classic) and maintained cordial relations with Tamil Nadu. Portuguese traveler Domingo Paes, who visited Vijayanagara around 1520 CE, described the capital Hampi as "the best-provided city in the world" with a population of perhaps half a million.

**Battle of Talikota (1565 CE):**
The catastrophic defeat of the Vijayanagara Empire by the Deccan Sultanate confederacy at the Battle of Talikota (also called Raksasa-Tangadi) effectively ended Vijayanagara imperial power, though the dynasty continued in reduced form. This battle's consequences for Tamil Nadu were significant: the Nayak governors, who had been appointed subordinates, now became de facto independent rulers.

## Who were the Nayaks and how did they transform Tamil Nadu?

Following the collapse of Vijayanagara central authority after 1565 CE, the Nayak governors of Tamil Nadu established independent kingdoms. The major Nayak kingdoms in Tamil Nadu were:

**Madurai Nayaks (1529–1736 CE):**
The most powerful and culturally significant of the Tamil Nayak kingdoms. Founded by Viswanatha Nayak, the Madurai Nayaks transformed Madurai into the cultural capital of Tamil Nadu:

**Viswanatha Nayak (1529–1564 CE):** Reorganized Tamil Nadu's society through the Paalaiyakkarar (poligar/palaiyam) system — dividing the territory into 72 paalaiyams (chieftaincies) each responsible for maintaining soldiers and paying tribute, while being autonomous in local administration. This system of distributed military-administrative control persisted until the British period.

**Thirumalai Nayak (1623–1659 CE):** The greatest Madurai Nayak ruler, famous for his enormous building projects. His major achievements:
- **Thirumalai Nayak Mahal (Palace):** Built in 1636 CE, this Indo-Saracenic palace in Madurai (combining Dravidian and Islamic architectural elements) still stands and is one of Tamil Nadu's major heritage monuments. Only one quarter of the original palace survives — the rest was demolished by Rani Mangammal to use materials elsewhere.
- **Expansion of the Meenakshi Amman Temple:** The current gopurams of the Meenakshi temple, including the iconic towers visible for miles around Madurai, were substantially built or rebuilt during Thirumalai Nayak's reign.
- **War with Thanjavur Nayaks and Mysore:** Thirumalai Nayak's reign was marked by continuous warfare; he was at various times a refugee dependent on the Thanjavur Nayaks.

**Rani Mangammal (1689–1706 CE):** One of the most capable rulers of medieval Tamil Nadu — a woman who served as regent for her grandson and governed effectively for nearly two decades. She is credited with administrative reforms, famine relief measures, and infrastructure development.

**Thanjavur Nayaks (1532–1673 CE):**
The Nayaks of Thanjavur, beginning with Sevappa Nayak, ruled the fertile Kaveri delta. Their most notable contribution was the patronage of Telugu literature and the Bharatanatyam dance tradition. The Thanjavur Nayak period is significant for:
- Development of the musical tradition that would culminate in the Carnatic music trinity of the late 18th–early 19th century (Tyagaraja, Muthuswami Dikshitar, Syama Sastri — all from the Thanjavur region).
- Continuation of the Chola bronze casting tradition.
- The Saraswati Mahal Library in Thanjavur, established by the Nayaks and expanded by the Maratha Bhonsle kings, which contains over 30,000 palm leaf manuscripts and is one of the oldest surviving manuscript libraries in Asia.

**Thanjavur Marathas (1674–1855 CE):**
The Thanjavur Nayak dynasty was replaced by the Maratha Bhonsle family under Venkoji (Ekoji), half-brother of the great Maratha king Shivaji, who conquered Thanjavur in 1674 CE. The Thanjavur Marathas patronized Tamil, Telugu, Sanskrit, and Marathi literature and music. King Serfoji II (1798–1832 CE) was a remarkable polymath — fluent in multiple languages, a serious botanist and physician, who modernized his court's library (the Saraswati Mahal) and corresponded with European scholars.

**Gingee (Senji) Nayaks and Fort:**
The Gingee Fort (Senji Kottai) in Villupuram district, built and expanded by the Vijayanagara Nayaks, is one of the most extraordinary military fortifications in South Asia. Called the "Troy of the East" by the Mughal general Zulfiqar Khan, it consists of three hills — Rajagiri, Krishnagiri, and Chandragiri — fortified with multiple concentric walls, underground granaries, and water tanks. It withstood an eight-year siege by the Mughal emperor Aurangzeb's forces (1689–1698 CE) under the Maratha general Rajaram (son of Shivaji). The fort later passed to the French, British, and then independent India.

## What were the cultural contributions of the Nayak period?

**Carnatic music foundations:**
The Nayak period was crucial for the development of Carnatic classical music — the South Indian classical music tradition. The Nayak courts attracted musicians from across South India; the raga-tala system was codified; and new compositions in Telugu, Tamil, and Sanskrit were created. The foundation was laid for the extraordinary musical flowering of the Thanjavur region in the 18th–19th centuries.

**Bharatanatyam:**
The classical dance form now known as Bharatanatyam (formerly called Sadir or Dasiattam) was developed and refined in the Nayak period, performed by devadasi (temple dancer) communities attached to major temples. The dance form integrated the abhinaya (expressive) tradition of Natyashastra with Tamil Bhakti poetry and Carnatic music. It was revived and reconstructed in the 20th century by E. Krishna Iyer and Rukmini Devi Arundale.

**Tamil literary renaissance:**
The Nayak period saw a renaissance of Tamil literature after the disruptions of the Sultanate period. The Kanda Puranam (Tamil version of Shiva Purana dealing with Murugan) was composed during this period. Kumara Guruparar, a prolific Tamil poet and Shaiva saint, was active during the Madurai Nayak period.

**Temple renovation and construction:**
The Nayak rulers invested enormously in temple renovation and construction across Tamil Nadu — not only in their capitals but throughout their territories. The distinctive feature of Nayak temple architecture is the enormous, highly decorated gopuram (gateway tower) covered with stucco figures of gods, humans, animals, and mythological scenes — creating a visual encyclopedia of Hindu mythology. The gopurams of Srirangam, Chidambaram, Madurai, Tiruvannamalai, Rameswaram, and Kumbakonam were substantially built or enlarged during the Nayak period.
