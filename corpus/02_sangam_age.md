# The Sangam Age: Tamil Civilization at Its Classical Peak

## What is the Sangam Age and when did it occur?

The Sangam Age refers to the period of classical Tamil civilization roughly dated from 300 BCE to 300 CE, though recent archaeological evidence from Keezhadi pushes its roots earlier to at least 600 BCE. The term "Sangam" derives from the legendary literary academies (Tamil: சங்கம், Cankam) said to have been convened in Madurai under the patronage of the Pandya kings. Three such Sangams are described in later Tamil tradition:

- **First Sangam (Muthal Sangam):** Said to have lasted 4,400 years, presided over by gods and legendary sages, with the city of Madurai submerged beneath the sea at its conclusion. This is understood as mythological.
- **Second Sangam (Idai Sangam):** Said to have lasted 3,700 years in a city called Kapadapuram, also eventually submerged.
- **Third Sangam (Kadai Sangam):** Held in Madurai, producing the corpus of literature we now call Sangam literature. This is the historically grounded period.

The Sangam corpus comprises approximately 2,381 poems by 473 named poets, including 30 women poets, compiled into anthologies between approximately 300 BCE and 300 CE. Its existence was rediscovered only in the 19th century by U. V. Swaminatha Iyer, who rescued manuscripts from destruction.

## What are the major texts of Sangam literature?

Sangam literature is organized into two broad categories — the Eight Anthologies (Ettuthokai) and the Ten Idylls (Pattupattu) — along with the didactic Thirukkural and grammatical Tolkappiyam:

**The Eight Anthologies (Ettuthokai):**
1. **Purananuru** — 400 poems on war, heroism, death, and the generosity of kings. Among the most historically valuable texts, naming kings, battles, and places.
2. **Akananuru** — 400 poems on love and nature, organized by the Akam (interior/love) convention.
3. **Natrinai** — 400 poems of the Akam genre, considered among the finest nature poetry in any language.
4. **Kuruntokai** — 401 short love poems, including the famous "What He Said" and "What She Said" poems that reveal the complexity of early Tamil gender relations.
5. **Kalittokai** — 150 poems in the Kali meter, covering all five Tinai landscapes.
6. **Ainkurunuru** — 500 very short poems, 100 per Tinai landscape.
7. **Paripadal** — Originally 70 poems, only 22 survive; focused on the gods Vishnu, Murugan, and the Vaigai river.
8. **Patirruppattu** — 80 surviving poems (of 100) praising the Chera kings.

**The Ten Idylls (Pattupattu):**
Include Tirumurugattrupadai (a guide to Murugan shrines), Porunarattrupadai, Cirupanatrupadai, Perumpanatrupadai, Mullaippattu, Maduraikkanji (a detailed description of Madurai city), Nedunathokai, Kurincippattu, Pattinappalai (describing the Chola port city of Kaveripattinam), and Malaipadukadam.

**Tolkappiyam:**
The oldest extant Tamil grammar text, attributed to Tolkappiyar. It covers phonology (Eluttatikaram), vocabulary and poetics (Collatikaram), and subject matter/poetics (Porulatikaram). Scholars debate its dating — some place it as early as 3rd century BCE, others as late as 5th century CE. The Tolkappiyam's Porulatikaram codifies the Akam-Puram (interior-exterior) classification and the Five Tinai landscape system that underpins all Sangam poetry.

**Thirukkural:**
Attributed to Thiruvalluvar, the Thirukkural comprises 1,330 couplets (kurals) organized into 133 chapters of 10 couplets each, covering three books: Aram (virtue/dharma), Porul (wealth/polity), and Inbam (love). Dated variously between 300 BCE and 500 CE, it has been translated into over 80 languages and is considered one of the greatest works of world literature. UNESCO recognized the Thirukkural in its world heritage of literature. Its ethical philosophy — non-violence, truthfulness, hospitality, compassion — transcends religion and is claimed by Hindus, Jains, and rationalists alike.

## What was the political structure of Sangam Age Tamil Nadu?

The Sangam Age was dominated by three major royal lineages and numerous minor chieftains:

**The Three Crowned Kings (Moovendhar):**

1. **Cheras (Keralaputra):** Based in present-day Kerala and parts of western Tamil Nadu, with capitals at Vanchi (identified with Karur or Muzris). The Cheras controlled the western spice trade routes — pepper, cardamom, ginger — which connected Tamil Nadu to the Roman Empire, Arab world, and Southeast Asia. Their emblem was the bow.

2. **Cholas:** Based in the fertile Kaveri delta, with the ancient capital at Uraiyur (present-day Tiruchirappalli) and the port city of Kaveripattinam (Poompuhar). The Cholas controlled the rich agricultural heartland of Tamil Nadu and the eastern seaboard trade. Their emblem was the tiger.

3. **Pandyas:** Based in the southernmost tip of India, with capital at Madurai. The Pandyas controlled the pearl fisheries of the Gulf of Mannar and the trade routes to Sri Lanka. Their emblem was the fish.

**The Minor Chieftains (Velir):**
Below the three crowned kings were numerous chieftains (Velir) who ruled smaller territories. Sangam literature celebrates their generosity (kodai) extensively — poets traveled between courts receiving gifts of gold, horses, elephants, and land grants. Notable Velir include Pari (of Parambu hills, known for gifting his chariot to a jasmine vine), Kari, Ay, Nalli, and Ori.

## What was the Akam-Puram classification system in Sangam poetry?

Sangam poetry is organized by a sophisticated classificatory system unique in world literature:

**Akam (அகம் — Interior/Love poetry):**
Akam poems deal exclusively with love and are entirely symbolic — no named persons, places, or historical events. Instead, they use a system of Five Tinai (landscapes) each associated with a phase of love:

| Tinai | Landscape | Flora | Fauna | Phase of Love | Season |
|-------|-----------|-------|-------|---------------|--------|
| Kurinji | Mountains | Kurinji flower | Elephant | Union/Courtship | Cool season |
| Mullai | Forest | Mullai jasmine | Deer | Patient waiting | Rainy season |
| Marutam | Farmland | Marutam tree | Buffalo | Infidelity/Reconciliation | All seasons |
| Neytal | Seashore | Blue lily | Stork | Separation/Longing | All seasons |
| Palai | Desert/Wasteland | Palai tree | Tiger | Separation/Elopement | Summer |

This system — called Tinai or Ullurai — is the primary analytical framework of Tolkappiyam's Porulatikaram and represents a sophisticated mapping of human emotional states onto the natural world. No other ancient literary tradition has produced a comparable system.

**Puram (புறம் — Exterior/Public poetry):**
Puram poems deal with public life — war, heroism, the ethics of kingship, death, and the poet's relationship with patrons. They are historically grounded, naming real kings, warriors, battles, and places. The Purananuru is the primary Puram anthology.

## What do we know about trade and economy in the Sangam Age?

The Sangam Age Tamil kingdoms were deeply integrated into a vast Indian Ocean trading network that connected them to Rome, Greece, Arabia, Persia, Southeast Asia, and China:

**Evidence of Roman trade:**
- Hoards of Roman gold coins (aurei and denarii) found throughout Tamil Nadu — at Coimbatore, Madurai, Karur, and dozens of other sites
- Roman amphora sherds and arretine ware (fine Roman tableware) found at Arikamedu (near Pondicherry), excavated by Sir Mortimer Wheeler in 1945 and identified as a Roman trading post
- The Roman text Periplus of the Erythraean Sea (c. 60–70 CE) and Ptolemy's Geography name Tamil ports including Muziris (Chera), Kaveripattinam (Chola), and Korkai (Pandya)
- Pliny the Elder complained in his Natural History (77 CE) that Rome was losing 50 million sesterces annually to India, much of it for pepper and other spices from the Tamil region

**Major exports from Tamil Nadu:**
Pepper, cardamom, ginger, turmeric, ivory, pearls (from Gulf of Mannar — among the finest in the ancient world), tortoiseshell, cotton textiles (muslin), iron, steel, precious stones (beryl, sapphire), and teak wood.

**Major imports:**
Gold, silver, wine, copper, tin, lead, and luxury goods.

**Port cities:**
- **Kaveripattinam/Poompuhar:** The Chola port, described in detail in the Pattinappalai as a city of immense wealth with a sophisticated harbor, warehouses, and a cosmopolitan population including foreign merchants.
- **Korkai:** The Pandya pearl port on the Tamiraparani river estuary, now silted up inland.
- **Tondi and Musiri:** Chera ports on the Malabar coast.
- **Arikamedu:** The Indo-Roman trading station near Pondicherry.

## Who were the notable poets and figures of the Sangam Age?

**Avvaiyar (First):** One of the most celebrated Tamil poets, traditionally depicted as an elderly woman of low caste who traveled between courts. Her poems in Purananuru display extraordinary moral clarity and political wisdom. Multiple "Avvaiyar" figures appear across Tamil literary history — the Sangam Avvaiyar is distinct from the later medieval Avvaiyar who wrote Aathichudi and Konraiventhan.

**Kapilar:** A Brahmin poet who was the intimate friend and eulogist of the chieftain Pari. His lament over Pari's death and his efforts to find husbands for Pari's daughters are among the most moving passages in Sangam literature. He is said to have fasted to death after Pari's fall.

**Thiruvalluvar:** Author of the Thirukkural. Tradition places him in Mylapore (Chennai). His caste identity has been debated across centuries — claimed by Brahmins, Vellalas, Dalits, and Jains. The Thirukkural's universal ethics suggest a deliberate transcendence of caste categories.

**Ilango Adigal:** Prince of the Chera dynasty who renounced the throne to become a Jain monk and composed the Cilappatikaram (The Tale of the Anklet) — considered one of the five great Tamil epics. The Cilappatikaram narrates the story of Kannagi and Kovalan across the three Sangam kingdoms, providing invaluable detail about urban life, trade, religion, and geography.

**Nakkirar:** A prominent Sangam poet credited with extensive commentary on Tolkappiyam and author of the Tirumurugattrupadai. Legend holds that he disputed even with the god Murugan over poetic correctness.
