# Tamil Language: The Oldest Living Classical Language

## What is the antiquity and status of the Tamil language?

Tamil is one of the world's oldest living languages and the only classical language of antiquity that remains a spoken vernacular with an unbroken literary tradition. Key facts about Tamil's antiquity:

- **Oldest attested Dravidian language:** Tamil is the oldest member of the Dravidian language family — which includes Telugu, Kannada, Malayalam, Tulu, Gondi, and dozens of other languages spoken primarily in South and Central India.
- **Literary documentation:** Tamil has a documented literary history of approximately 2,300 years — from the earliest Tamil-Brahmi inscriptions (3rd century BCE) to the Sangam corpus (300 BCE – 300 CE) to the unbroken literary tradition continuing to the present day.
- **Classical language recognition:** The Government of India recognized Tamil as a "Classical Language" in 2004 — the first language to receive this designation — based on criteria including high antiquity, a large body of ancient literature, and an independent tradition not derived from another language.
- **Living language:** Tamil is spoken by approximately 80 million people as a first language — in Tamil Nadu (approximately 63 million), Sri Lanka (approximately 3 million), Malaysia (approximately 2 million), Singapore (approximately 500,000), and diaspora communities worldwide.

## What is the Tolkappiyam and why is it significant?

The Tolkappiyam is the oldest surviving Tamil grammatical text and one of the oldest grammatical treatises in any language. Attributed to the grammarian Tolkappiyar, it is dated variously between the 3rd century BCE and 5th century CE — most scholars place its composition (or at least its earliest layers) in the early Sangam period.

**Structure of Tolkappiyam:**
The text is divided into three books (adhikarams):

1. **Eluttatikaram (Phonology):** Describes the Tamil phonological system — vowels (uyir), consonants (mey), and compound letters. The Tamil alphabetic system described is among the most phonologically systematic in any ancient language — distinguishing short and long vowels, nasal consonants, and various categories of stops with great precision.

2. **Collatikaram (Grammar of words):** Covers morphology — the classification of nouns, verbs, and other word classes, including the highly complex system of Tamil nominal case suffixes and verbal conjugation. Tamil's agglutinative morphology (where multiple suffixes are added to roots to form complex words) is described with analytical sophistication comparable to Panini's Sanskrit grammar (Ashtadhyayi).

3. **Porulatikaram (Subject matter and poetics):** The most culturally extraordinary section — it codifies the entire system of Akam (love poetry) and Puram (public poetry), including the Five Tinai landscape system, the symbolic vocabulary of imagery, the appropriate characters and situations for each genre, and the ethical norms governing literary representation. Nothing comparable exists in any other ancient literary tradition.

**Uniqueness of Tolkappiyam:**
What makes Tolkappiyam extraordinary in world linguistic history is that it treats language not merely phonologically and morphologically (as Sanskrit grammars do) but also as a vehicle of aesthetic and emotional meaning — integrating linguistics, poetics, and cultural semiotics in a single framework.

## What is the history of Tamil script?

The Tamil script has a documented evolution spanning over two millennia:

**Tamil-Brahmi (3rd century BCE – 5th century CE):**
The earliest Tamil writing used an adapted form of the Brahmi script — called Tamil-Brahmi or Tamili — which modified the standard Brahmi to accommodate Tamil phonological features (particularly the retroflex and alveolar distinctions of Tamil that do not exist in Sanskrit). Tamil-Brahmi inscriptions are found on cave walls throughout Tamil Nadu (Madurai, Tirunelveli districts particularly) and on pottery sherds at Keezhadi and other sites. These inscriptions — recording the names of donors, property transactions, and occasionally literary verses — document an already literate, widespread Tamil-speaking civilization in the 3rd–2nd centuries BCE.

**Vatteluttu (c. 3rd–13th century CE):**
A cursive script used in Kerala and parts of southern Tamil Nadu, derived from Tamil-Brahmi. Vatteluttu was used for many Tamil and early Malayalam texts before the modern Tamil and Malayalam scripts stabilized.

**Modern Tamil script:**
The current Tamil script evolved from Tamil-Brahmi through intermediate forms. It was substantially standardized by the 7th–8th century CE. The modern script has:
- 12 vowels (uyir)
- 18 consonants (mey)
- 216 compound characters (uyirmey) — each consonant combined with each vowel
- Plus 1 special character (āytam)
= 247 characters total

The Tamil script is notable for:
- A systematic phonological organization that reflects the actual sound system of classical Tamil with great accuracy
- No capital letters
- A distinctive aesthetic that is fully angular and round simultaneously — often described as uniquely beautiful

## What are the major periods and genres of Tamil literature?

**Sangam Literature (300 BCE – 300 CE):** Already discussed. The foundational corpus.

**Post-Sangam Epics (c. 200–600 CE):**
The five great Tamil epics (Aimperumkappiyam):
- **Cilappatikaram** (Ilango Adigal): The Tale of the Anklet — the story of Kannagi, Kovalan, and Madhavi across the three Tamil kingdoms. Combines realistic description with religious mysticism.
- **Manimekalai** (Sattanar): A Buddhist sequel to Cilappatikaram, following Kovalan's daughter Manimekalai who becomes a Buddhist nun. The only surviving major Buddhist Tamil literary text.
- **Civaka Cintamani** (Tiruttakka Tevar): A Jain romance epic — the story of the hero Civakan's many loves and final renunciation. The longest of the five epics.
- **Valayapati and Kundalakesi:** Only fragments survive.

**Devotional Literature — Bhakti (6th–9th century CE):**
The Nayanmars (63 Shaiva saints) and Alvars (12 Vaishnava saints) composed devotional hymns that revolutionized Tamil religious culture. The collected Nayanar hymns — called Tirumurai (12 books) — include:
- **Tevaram** (Books 1–7): Hymns of Thirugnana Sambandar, Thirunavukkarasar (Appar), and Sundarar — the three principal Nayanmars.
- **Tiruvacakam** (Book 8): Hymns of Manikkavacakar — widely considered the most sublime devotional poetry in Tamil, combining intense personal mysticism with great lyric beauty.
- **Tirumurai books 9–12:** Hymns of other Nayanmars and the Periyapuranam (hagiographies).

The Alvar corpus — the Nalayira Divya Prabandham (4,000 Divine Verses) — is the Vaishnava equivalent, expressing an intensely personal devotional relationship with Vishnu/Krishna across all 12 Alvars.

**Medieval period (10th–16th century CE):**
- **Kamba Ramayanam** (Kamban, c. 12th century): The Tamil Ramayana — a creative reimagining of Valmiki's text that is considered the greatest single work of medieval Tamil literature.
- **Puranas:** Tamil versions of Sanskrit Puranas — Sekkizhar's Periyapuranam, Umapati Sivacharya's theological works.
- **Devotional poetry:** Numerous poets in the Bhakti tradition.

**Modern Tamil literature (19th century – present):**
- **Bharathiyar (Subramania Bharati, 1882–1921):** The revolutionary nationalist poet who transformed Tamil poetry — bringing passionate political energy, feminist themes, and modernist free verse into Tamil. His patriotic songs ("Vande Mataram" in Tamil, "Bharatha Samudayam Vaazhga") and feminist poems ("Pudhumai Penn" — The New Woman) remain among the most recited Tamil texts.
- **Novel and short story:** The Tamil novel tradition begins with Vedanayakam Pillai's Piratapa Mudaliar Charithiram (1879) — sometimes called the first Indian novel in any language. The 20th century produced major novelists: Kalki Krishnamurthy (Ponniyin Selvan — a historical novel about the Chola period, enormously popular and recently adapted to film by Mani Ratnam), Jayakanthan, Ashokamitran.
- **Contemporary Tamil literature:** A rich tradition of poetry, fiction, and drama continues, including Dalit Tamil literature (Bama's Karukku, Sivakami's The Grip of Change), feminist writing, and diaspora Tamil literature.
