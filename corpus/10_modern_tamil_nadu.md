# Modern Tamil Nadu: Post-Independence Development and Identity

## What happened to Tamil Nadu at Indian Independence in 1947?

Tamil Nadu (then called Madras State) became part of independent India on August 15, 1947. Key events of the transition period:

**C. Rajagopalachari (Rajaji, 1878–1972):**
The most prominent Tamil Congress leader — a close associate of Gandhi and the last Governor-General of India — Rajagopalachari was deeply controversial in Tamil Nadu. His support for Hindi imposition and his proposal for a hereditary educational system (the "Kula Kalvi Thittam" — where children would learn their parents' trade rather than receive general education) were seen as regressive and anti-Dalit. These positions drove many Tamil intellectuals and activists away from Congress toward the Dravidian parties.

**States Reorganization (1956):**
The States Reorganisation Act of 1956 redrew India's internal boundaries on linguistic lines. Madras State lost the Telugu-speaking northern districts (which became part of Andhra Pradesh) and some Kannada-speaking areas (which went to Karnataka/Mysore). The Malayalam-speaking areas had already been separated as Travancore-Cochin in 1949.

The borders of the newly reorganized Madras State — renamed Tamil Nadu in 1969 — roughly correspond to the geographical distribution of the Tamil-speaking population.

**Cauvery water dispute:**
The sharing of Cauvery river waters between Tamil Nadu and Karnataka has been a source of ongoing conflict since the 19th century. The Cauvery is the lifeline of the Kaveri delta (Thanjavur, Tiruvarur, Nagapattinam districts) — the most productive agricultural zone in Tamil Nadu. The dispute over how much water Karnataka can impound and how much must flow to Tamil Nadu has led to repeated inter-state conflicts, Supreme Court interventions, and the Cauvery Water Disputes Tribunal (established 1990, final award 2007).

## What is the significance of the Tamil Eelam issue?

The Tamil Eelam conflict in Sri Lanka (1983–2009) was deeply felt in Tamil Nadu and had significant political consequences:

**Background:**
The Tamil-speaking population of Sri Lanka (approximately 15–18% of the island's population) faced systematic discrimination following independence, including exclusion from state employment, restrictions on education, and the 1956 Sinhala Only Act making Sinhala the sole official language. Progressive Tamil demands for federalism escalated to armed conflict when the Liberation Tigers of Tamil Eelam (LTTE) launched an insurgency for a separate Tamil homeland in the north and east of Sri Lanka.

**Tamil Nadu's response:**
The Tamil Eelam issue created enormous popular sympathy in Tamil Nadu — cutting across party lines. All major Tamil parties demanded Indian intervention on behalf of Sri Lankan Tamils. The Indian government's involvement included:
- Training and arming Tamil militant groups in the early 1980s (under Indira Gandhi's government)
- The Indo-Sri Lanka Accord of 1987 (under Rajiv Gandhi) and the deployment of the Indian Peace Keeping Force (IPKF) — which ended disastrously
- Rajiv Gandhi was assassinated in 1991 in Sriperumbudur, Tamil Nadu, by an LTTE suicide bomber — an event that created complex feelings in Tamil Nadu

The LTTE was militarily defeated by the Sri Lankan Army in May 2009, with the final battle causing civilian casualties that created deep anguish in Tamil Nadu and internationally.

## What is Tamil Nadu's economic profile?

Tamil Nadu is one of India's most industrialized and economically developed states:

**Economic ranking:** Tamil Nadu has the second-largest state economy in India (after Maharashtra) as measured by Gross State Domestic Product. Its per capita income is among the highest of India's major states.

**Industrial base:**
- **Automobiles:** Tamil Nadu is India's largest automobile manufacturing state — Chennai is called the "Detroit of India." Major manufacturers including Hyundai, Ford, Renault-Nissan, BMW, Ashok Leyland, Royal Enfield, and TVS have major plants in Tamil Nadu.
- **Textiles:** The Tiruppur cluster (Coimbatore district) is one of the world's largest knitwear export clusters — producing approximately $3 billion worth of knitwear annually for global brands. Coimbatore itself is a major textile machinery and engineering hub.
- **Software and IT:** Chennai is one of India's major IT/software export hubs, home to the Indian operations of major multinationals including Cognizant, Infosys, Wipro, TCS, Ford Global Technology, Saint-Gobain, and many others.
- **Leather:** Tamil Nadu (particularly Vellore, Chennai, and Ranipet districts) produces approximately 40% of India's leather exports.
- **Engineering:** Coimbatore is a major center for pump manufacturing, foundries, and precision engineering.

**Agriculture:**
Despite industrialization, agriculture remains important — Tamil Nadu produces significant quantities of rice (Kaveri delta), bananas (Theni, Trichy districts), turmeric (Erode — the "Turmeric City"), jasmine, coconuts, and mangoes.

## What are the major cultural contributions of Tamil Nadu in the modern era?

**Carnatic music:**
The Carnatic classical music tradition, centered in Tamil Nadu, reached its apex in the late 18th–early 19th century with the Trinity of Carnatic music:
- **Tyagaraja (1767–1847):** Composed approximately 700 krithis (compositions), primarily in Telugu with some in Sanskrit. His compositions — particularly the Pancharatna Krithis — are the core repertoire of Carnatic music. Born in Tiruvarur, worked in Thiruvaiyaru (Thanjavur district).
- **Muthuswami Dikshitar (1775–1835):** Composed approximately 450 krithis, primarily in Sanskrit with significant Mantra content. His compositions demonstrate the most sophisticated harmonic and structural complexity in Carnatic music.
- **Syama Sastri (1762–1827):** The oldest of the Trinity, composed approximately 300 works. Known for their emotional depth.

The Madras Music Academy (founded 1928) and the annual Music Season in Chennai (December–January) — where hundreds of classical music and dance performances are held — make Chennai one of the world's great centers of classical performing arts.

**Tamil cinema:**
Tamil cinema (Kollywood) is one of the world's major film industries, with a history dating to 1916 (the first Tamil silent film was Keechaka Vadham). Modern Tamil cinema is known for its scale, technical sophistication, and cultural influence across South Asia and among the Tamil diaspora:
- **S. S. Vasan's Chandralekha (1948):** The first Tamil film to achieve pan-Indian distribution.
- **Parasakthi (1952):** Written by M. Karunanidhi, this film — with explicitly Dravidianist themes — launched the political careers of both Karunanidhi and actor Sivaji Ganesan.
- **Contemporary Tamil cinema:** Directors like Mani Ratnam (Nayakan, Roja, Bombay, Ponniyin Selvan), K. Balachander, Shankar, Pa. Ranjith, and Mari Selvaraj have created works of international significance. Composers A. R. Rahman (Oscar winner for Slumdog Millionaire score) and Ilaiyaraaja have achieved global recognition.

**Tamil diaspora:**
The Tamil diaspora — estimated at 4–5 million people outside India and Sri Lanka — has significant communities in:
- **Malaysia and Singapore:** Descendants of indentured laborers brought by the British to work on rubber plantations and in tin mines; now substantial communities maintaining Tamil language and culture.
- **Sri Lanka:** The Sri Lankan Tamil community, concentrated in the north and east.
- **UK, USA, Canada, Australia:** Post-1960s professional and student migration creating successful diaspora communities.
- **Gulf States:** Large labor migration community.

The Tamil diaspora maintains strong cultural connections — Tamil language schools, temples, cultural associations — and has been a significant factor in international advocacy on Tamil Eelam and other issues.

## What is the Tamil Nadu model of welfare politics?

Tamil Nadu developed a distinctive model of state-level welfare politics that has become influential nationally:

**Midday Meal Scheme:** Initiated by K. Kamaraj in 1956 and massively expanded by MGR, Tamil Nadu's school meal program achieved near-universal implementation. Studies have shown it to be one of the most effective interventions for improving school enrollment, attendance, and child nutrition in India.

**Universal Public Distribution System:** Tamil Nadu maintains one of India's most comprehensive subsidized food distribution systems, providing rice at nominal prices to all families.

**Health indicators:** Tamil Nadu has among the best public health indicators in India — low infant and maternal mortality, high institutional delivery rates, and relatively good access to healthcare. The Tamil Nadu Health Systems Project has been cited as a model of pharmaceutical procurement and healthcare delivery.

**Women's status:** Tamil Nadu has historically had relatively better indicators for women's status compared to North Indian states — higher female literacy, lower sex ratio distortion at birth, higher female workforce participation. The Dravidian movement's early emphasis on women's rights (Periyar's self-respect marriages, widow remarriage advocacy) created a cultural foundation for these outcomes.
