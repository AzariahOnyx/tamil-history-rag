# The Pallava Dynasty: Architecture, Sanskrit, and Tamil Synthesis

## Who were the Pallavas and where did they originate?

The Pallava dynasty ruled from approximately 275 CE to 897 CE, with their capital at Kanchipuram (also called Kanchi), one of the seven sacred cities of Hinduism. The origin of the Pallavas is contested among historians:

- **Foreign origin theory:** Some early scholars proposed that the Pallavas were descended from the Pahlavas (Parthians) of Iran, based on linguistic similarity. This theory is now largely rejected.
- **Local chieftain theory:** Most contemporary historians believe the Pallavas were a local dynasty that rose from the position of subordinate chiefs under the Satavahana empire. When the Satavahanas declined in the 3rd century CE, the Pallavas asserted independence in the eastern Deccan and northern Tamil Nadu.
- **Brahmin origin theory:** Tamil tradition sometimes connects the Pallavas to a Brahmin lineage through mythological genealogies.

The Pallavas initially used Prakrit in their inscriptions (3rd–4th century CE), then shifted to Sanskrit (5th–7th century CE), and finally incorporated Tamil alongside Sanskrit — a reflection of their gradual integration into Tamil cultural life.

## What was the political history of the Pallava dynasty?

**Early Pallavas (275–550 CE):**
The earliest Pallava records are copper plate grants in Prakrit. Rulers like Sivaskandavarman issued grants from Kanchipuram. This period saw the Pallavas consolidating control over the Tamil Velir chieftains and competing with the Kadambas of Karnataka and the Vakatakas of the Deccan.

**Imperial Pallavas — the great kings:**

**Simhavishnu (575–600 CE):** The first great imperial Pallava, Simhavishnu conquered the Kalabhras (mysterious rulers who had disrupted Tamil Nadu for nearly 300 years in what is called the "Dark Age" of Tamil history) and established firm Pallava control over northern Tamil Nadu. He was a devotee of Vishnu.

**Mahendravarman I (600–630 CE):** One of the most remarkable rulers in South Indian history — a scholar, poet, musician, playwright, and architect. His Sanskrit drama Mattavilasaprahasana (The Farce of the Drunkard) is a comic masterpiece satirizing Buddhist monks, Shaiva ascetics, and Kapalikas. He began as a Jain and converted to Shaivism under the influence of the saint Appar. Most significantly, Mahendravarman I pioneered rock-cut architecture in Tamil Nadu — the mandapa (pillared hall) style of cave temples at Mahabalipuram and throughout his kingdom. Before his reign, structural temples in Tamil Nadu were built of perishable materials; he initiated the transition to stone.

**Narasimhavarman I Mahamalla (630–668 CE):** The greatest military Pallava. He defeated and humiliated the Chalukya king Pulakesi II (who had previously defeated Mahendravarman) three times, sacking the Chalukya capital Vatapi (Badami) in 642 CE — earning the title "Vatapikonda" (Conqueror of Vatapi). He also sent two naval expeditions to Sri Lanka to restore the Sinhalese king Manavarma to the throne. Under his patronage, Mahabalipuram (Mamallapuram) was developed as both a port city and an extraordinary open-air sculpture gallery. The rathas (chariot-temples) and the Shore Temple were commissioned in his reign.

**Narasimhavarman II Rajasimha (695–728 CE):** The great temple builder. Under him, the Kailasanatha Temple at Kanchipuram — the first fully structural (as opposed to rock-cut) major temple in Tamil Nadu — was completed. This temple is a watershed in South Indian architectural history. He also completed the Shore Temple at Mahabalipuram.

**Nandivarman II Pallavamalla (731–796 CE):** Came to power through election by ministers after the main royal line died out. His long reign saw continuous warfare with the Chalukyas, Rashtrakutas, and the rising Pandyas and Gangas. The Vaikunta Perumal Temple at Kanchipuram was built during his reign.

**Decline and fall:** The Pallava dynasty was progressively weakened by the rising Pandyas under Srimara Srivallabha and ultimately destroyed by the Chola king Aditya I, who killed the last significant Pallava ruler Aparajita around 897 CE.

## What is the architectural legacy of the Pallavas?

Pallava architecture represents one of the most significant transitions in Indian art history — from rock-cut to structural temple architecture, and the development of the Dravidian (specifically the Tamil) temple style:

**Phase 1 — Mahendra style (early 7th century):**
Simple rock-cut mandapas (pillared halls) with minimal ornamentation. The pillars have a distinctive "lion base" (vyali base). Examples: Mandagapattu cave temple (where Mahendravarman I claims to have built a temple without brick, timber, metal, or mortar — i.e., in rock), Mahendravadi, Siyamangalam.

**Phase 2 — Mamalla style (mid-7th century):**
More elaborate rock-cut work and the transition to monolithic structural forms. The Five Rathas (Pancha Pandava Rathas) at Mahabalipuram are the masterpieces — five monolithic temples carved from a single granite outcrop, each in a different architectural style (representing different regional temple forms). The Arjuna's Penance/Descent of the Ganges bas-relief — 27 meters wide and 9 meters tall — is the largest open-air bas-relief in the world, with over 100 figures including gods, humans, animals, and celestials.

**Phase 3 — Rajasimha style (late 7th–early 8th century):**
Full structural temples with elaborate iconographic programs. The Kailasanatha Temple at Kanchipuram (built c. 700–728 CE) is the paradigm — a multi-storey vimana (tower) over the sanctum, surrounding subsidiary shrines, and an extensive narrative sculptural program. Its architectural vocabulary directly influenced all subsequent Dravidian temple architecture.

**The Shore Temple at Mahabalipuram:**
Built by Narasimhavarman II around 700–728 CE, the Shore Temple is among the oldest surviving structural temples in South India. It comprises three shrines — two to Shiva and one to Vishnu (Anantashayana). Recent underwater archaeological surveys have discovered submerged structures offshore, lending credence to the legend of the "Seven Pagodas" of Mahabalipuram — six temples supposedly swallowed by the sea.

## What was the cultural and religious significance of the Pallava period?

**Bhakti movement origins:**
The Pallava period saw the emergence and early development of the Tamil Bhakti movement — the devotional religious revolution that transformed Hinduism across South Asia. The Nayanmars (63 Shaiva saints) and Alvars (12 Vaishnava saints) composed devotional hymns in Tamil that democratized religious access — breaking Brahminical Sanskrit monopoly on religious expression.

Key figures of the Pallava period:
- **Appar (Thirunavukkarasar):** A contemporary of Mahendravarman I, Appar converted from Jainism back to Shaivism and converted the king. His Tevaram hymns are among the earliest and most theologically sophisticated Tamil Bhakti compositions.
- **Thirugnana Sambandar:** A child prodigy saint who composed thousands of Tevaram hymns and is credited with converting the Pandya king from Jainism.
- **Periyalvar and Andal:** Vaishnava Alvars; Andal is the only woman among the twelve Alvars, and her Tiruppavai (30 verses sung during the Tamil month of Margazhi) remains one of the most recited texts in Tamil devotional life.

**Kanchipuram as a center of learning:**
The Pallava capital Kanchipuram was one of the great intellectual centers of medieval Asia. The Buddhist philosopher Dharmapala (c. 530–561 CE), who became head of Nalanda university, was born in Kanchipuram. The Chinese pilgrim Xuanzang (Hieun Tsang) visited Kanchipuram in 642 CE and described it as a prosperous city of great learning with numerous Buddhist monasteries alongside Hindu temples. The philosopher Dignaga, a major figure in Buddhist logic, is also connected to Kanchipuram.

**Sanskrit and Tamil synthesis:**
The Pallava period was crucial for the integration of Sanskrit Brahminical culture with indigenous Tamil traditions. The Pallavas promoted Sanskrit through royal inscriptions and temple building while Tamil Bhakti saints promoted Tamil as the language of devotion. This bilingual cultural synthesis — sometimes called "Sanskrit cosmopolis meets Tamil vernacular" — shaped the unique character of Tamil Hinduism.
