# The Dravidian Movement: Social Reform and Political Revolution

## What was the Dravidian movement and what were its origins?

The Dravidian movement is one of the most significant social and political revolutions in modern Indian history — a broad transformation of Tamil (and to some extent, broader South Indian) society that challenged caste hierarchy, Brahminical dominance, and North Indian cultural hegemony, and ultimately reshaped the political landscape of Tamil Nadu permanently.

Its roots lie in the late 19th and early 20th century social reform movements, but its organizational and ideological consolidation came through two figures: Periyar E. V. Ramasamy and C. N. Annadurai.

## Who was Periyar and what was the Self-Respect Movement?

**E. V. Ramasamy Naicker (1879–1973)**, known universally as Periyar (Tamil: "the great one/elder"), was born into a prosperous Naicker merchant family in Erode (then Madras Presidency). His intellectual journey moved from devout Hinduism to radical atheism and rationalism through a series of transformative experiences, most notably a visit to Kashi (Varanasi) where he experienced firsthand the humiliation meted out to non-Brahmin pilgrims.

**Key principles of Periyar's Self-Respect Movement (founded 1925):**
- **Rationalism and atheism:** Periyar rejected all religious authority — Hindu, Christian, Muslim — as instruments of social oppression. His newspaper Kudi Arasu and later Viduthalai propagated rationalist thought.
- **Anti-caste:** The varna system and untouchability were not merely social evils but were, in Periyar's analysis, tools of Brahminical economic and political domination. He called for the complete abolition of caste.
- **Women's liberation:** Periyar was one of the most radical voices for women's equality in 20th century India — advocating widow remarriage, divorce rights, birth control, and women's sexual autonomy. He conducted "Self-Respect Marriages" (without priests or religious rituals) from 1928 onward.
- **Anti-Brahminism:** Periyar distinguished between Brahmins as a community and Brahminism as an ideology of hierarchical dominance. His critique was structural rather than personal.
- **Tamil identity and Dravidian pride:** Periyar promoted the idea that Tamil/Dravidian civilization was older and in important ways superior to the Sanskrit-Aryan culture imposed from the north. He reinterpreted Hindu mythology — particularly the Ramayana — as allegories of Aryan conquest of Dravidian civilization (his "True Ramayana" or Ramayana Padi was enormously controversial).

**Periyar and Congress:**
Periyar was initially active in the Indian National Congress but resigned in 1925 after the Congress organization in Tamil Nadu maintained caste-based seating arrangements and served food prepared by Brahmins at a meeting. He never rejoined the nationalist mainstream, maintaining that Indian independence under Congress would merely replace British domination with Brahmin-Bania domination.

**Justice Party:**
The Non-Brahmin Manifesto of 1916, signed by prominent non-Brahmin leaders, led to the formation of the South Indian Liberal Federation (popularly called the Justice Party) in 1917. The Justice Party — which included wealthy non-Brahmin landlords and professionals — won elections to the Madras Legislative Council under the Montagu-Chelmsford reforms (1920) and implemented the first caste-based reservations in government employment in India (the Communal Government Order of 1921). Periyar later took control of the Justice Party (1938), renaming it Dravidar Kazhagam (DK) in 1944.

## Who was C. N. Annadurai and how was the DMK formed?

**Conjeevaram Natarajan Annadurai (1909–1969)**, known as Anna ("elder brother"), was Periyar's most brilliant disciple and the founder of the Dravida Munnetra Kazhagam (DMK — Dravidian Progress Federation).

Annadurai differed from Periyar in crucial ways:
- He was a constructive politician rather than a pure ideologue — interested in electoral politics and governance.
- He was a brilliant Tamil writer, playwright, and orator — his plays (particularly Chandrodhayam and Velaikkari) combined political messaging with literary quality.
- He was less absolute in his atheism and anti-Brahminism, building broader coalitions.
- He believed in working within the Indian constitutional framework for Tamil rights.

**Formation of DMK (1949):**
Annadurai broke with Periyar in 1949, primarily over Periyar's support for Pakistan and his late marriage to a much younger woman. He founded the DMK with a more explicitly political (rather than purely social reform) agenda. The DMK's key demands:
- **Anti-Hindi agitation:** Opposition to the imposition of Hindi as the national language of India, which was seen as cultural imperialism favoring North India.
- **State autonomy:** Greater power for the states versus the central government.
- **Social justice:** Continuation of reservations and anti-caste policies.
- Initially, **Dravida Nadu** (separate Dravidian homeland) — abandoned after the Chinese aggression of 1962 when Annadurai declared that the DMK would not press for separation during a national security crisis.

## What was the Hindi agitation and why was it significant?

The anti-Hindi agitations of Tamil Nadu are among the most significant mass movements in post-independence India:

**First Anti-Hindi Agitation (1937–1940):**
When the Congress government of C. Rajagopalachari (Rajaji) in Madras Presidency attempted to make Hindi compulsory in schools in 1937, massive protests erupted across Tamil Nadu. Over 1,000 people were imprisoned, and two protesters — Natarajan and Asari — died in police firing. The agitation was successful: the order was withdrawn in 1940.

**Second Anti-Hindi Agitation (1965):**
When the Indian constitution's provision making Hindi the sole official language of India was to come into effect on January 26, 1965, Tamil Nadu erupted in massive protests. The agitation was more violent than the first — approximately 70 people died (some accounts put the figure higher), including self-immolations. The Madras city witnessed riots, burning of public property, and strikes. The Congress government in Delhi was forced to pass the Official Languages Act of 1967, guaranteeing that English would continue as an associate official language as long as non-Hindi speaking states desired — effectively giving Tamil Nadu and other states a veto over Hindi imposition.

The 1965 agitation had a transformative political consequence: it swept the Congress government in Tamil Nadu from power and brought the DMK to office in the 1967 elections — the first time a regional party had won outright power in any Indian state. Tamil Nadu has never returned to Congress dominance since.

## What was the political evolution of Tamil Nadu after 1967?

**C. N. Annadurai (Chief Minister 1967–1969):**
Anna's brief tenure as Chief Minister (he died of cancer in 1969) was marked by symbolic gestures of Tamil cultural pride (changing the state name from "Madras State" to "Tamil Nadu" in 1969) and practical governance. His death at 60 was mourned by millions — his funeral cortege in Madras reportedly attracted 15 million people, one of the largest funeral gatherings in human history.

**M. Karunanidhi (Chief Minister multiple terms: 1969–71, 1971–76, 1989–91, 1996–2001, 2006–11):**
Muthuvel Karunanidhi, known as Kalaignar ("Artiste"), was Annadurai's political heir and one of the longest-serving and most influential politicians in Indian history. A screenwriter and playwright before becoming a politician, Karunanidhi was both a Tamil cultural champion and a consummate political operator. His key contributions:
- Strong advocate of Tamil language and culture.
- Championed Dravidian social justice politics.
- Led Tamil Nadu's federalist demands for greater state autonomy.
- Navigated the complex politics of the Tamil Eelam issue in Sri Lanka.

**M. G. Ramachandran — AIADMK (Chief Minister 1977–87):**
Marudur Gopalan Ramachandran, known universally as MGR, was the most beloved Tamil film star of his era who transitioned into politics, splitting from the DMK to form the All India Anna Dravida Munnetra Kazhagam (AIADMK) in 1972. His political success was built on his film persona as a champion of the poor and downtrodden. MGR implemented the pioneering Midday Meal Scheme — providing free noon meals to schoolchildren — which dramatically improved school attendance among poor children and became a model replicated across India.

**J. Jayalalithaa (Chief Minister multiple terms: 1991–96, 2001–06, 2011–14, 2015–16):**
MGR's protégée and film co-star who became the dominant political figure of Tamil Nadu after his death. Known as Amma ("mother"), Jayalalithaa was both highly autocratic and genuinely popular, combining welfarist politics with authoritarian governance. Her welfare schemes — free mixies and grinders, subsidized housing, free color television sets — defined a distinctive Tamil Nadu model of distributive politics.
