# The Pandya Dynasty: Pearls, Trade, and Southern Power

## Who were the Pandyas and what was their historical significance?

The Pandya dynasty ruled from the southernmost tip of the Indian subcontinent — the region around Madurai, Tirunelveli, and the Gulf of Mannar — for over two millennia, making them one of the oldest continuously documented royal dynasties in world history. The Pandyas are mentioned in:

- Ashoka's rock edicts (3rd century BCE) as a border kingdom beyond Mauryan influence
- The Sangam corpus (300 BCE – 300 CE) as one of the three crowned kings
- The Greek geographer Megasthenes (c. 300 BCE) who described the "Pandaei" as a kingdom ruled by a woman — possibly reflecting the matrilineal traditions of the region
- The Roman Periplus of the Erythraean Sea (c. 60 CE) as a major trading partner
- Medieval Arab geographers who described the pearl fisheries of the "Maabar" (Coromandel coast under Pandya rule)
- Marco Polo (c. 1292 CE) who visited the Pandya kingdom and described it in detail

**The Pandya emblem** was the fish — still used as a symbol in Tamil cultural contexts.

## What was the political history of the Pandya dynasty?

**Sangam Age Pandyas (300 BCE – 300 CE):**
The Sangam Pandyas ruled from Korkai (on the Tamiraparani estuary) and later Madurai. They patronized the legendary Tamil Sangam academies. The Purananuru records their wars against the Cholas and Cheras and celebrates their generosity. Nedunjeliyan (also called Arivudai Nambi) is the most celebrated Sangam Pandya king — he is the king who executed the innocent merchant Kovalan in Cilappatikaram, bringing the wrath of the goddess Kannagi upon his city.

**Dark Age (300–600 CE):**
The Kalabhras — a mysterious dynasty of uncertain origin (possibly from North India, possibly local) — disrupted the established order of Tamil Nadu during this period, displacing the three crowned kings. The Pandyas, like the Cholas, experienced a severe reduction in power during this period. Buddhist and Jain sources suggest the Kalabhras may have been patrons of non-Brahminical religions.

**Medieval Pandyas — first phase (600–920 CE):**
The Pandyas re-emerged under Kadungon, who is credited with defeating the Kalabhras. His successors — particularly Arikesari Maravarman (Parankusa Pandyan, a contemporary of the Nayanmars), Nedumaran (who debated with Thirugnana Sambandar before converting from Jainism to Shaivism), and Varagunavarman I — made the Pandya kingdom a major power rivaling the Pallavas.

Key conflict: The Pandyas and Pallavas were frequently at war. The Velvikkudi copper plate grant records Pandya victories over the Pallavas. Conversely, the Pallavas repeatedly intervened in Pandya succession disputes.

**The Three-Cornered Wars and Pandya apogee (9th century):**
Under Srimara Srivallabha (c. 815–862 CE), the Pandyas reached their greatest extent of power. Srimara:
- Invaded Sri Lanka and plundered the Buddhist sacred city of Anuradhapura, taking the sacred tooth relic and Buddha statue.
- Decisively defeated the Pallava king Nandivarman III.
- Launched raids into Chola territory.

His successors Varaguna II and Parantaka Viranarayana continued Pandya expansion, but overreach led to defeat by the Chola king Aditya I and the Pallava-Ganga alliance.

**Interregnum under Chola dominance (10th–13th century):**
The Pandyas were subdued by Parantaka Chola I (who captured Madurai) and remained under Chola suzerainty for most of this period, though they maintained a semi-independent existence and continued to produce notable cultural achievements.

**Second Pandya Empire — the great revival (1190–1310 CE):**
The later Pandyas, beginning with Maravarman Sundara Pandyan I, threw off Chola overlordship and established the most powerful Pandya state in history:

**Maravarman Sundara Pandyan I (1216–1238 CE):** Defeated the Cholas decisively and established Pandya independence.

**Maravarman Kulasekara Pandyan I (1268–1310 CE):** The greatest medieval Pandya king:
- Destroyed the last Chola king Rajendra III around 1279 CE, ending the Chola empire.
- Unified virtually all of Tamil Nadu under Pandya rule.
- Extended influence into Sri Lanka and Kerala.
- His kingdom was described in glowing terms by Marco Polo, who visited around 1292 CE and described the pearl fisheries and the extraordinary wealth of the Pandya court. Polo noted that the king wore only a loincloth but was covered in jewels worth more than a city.
- Manned a powerful navy; traded extensively with Arabia, Persia, China, and Southeast Asia.

**The Delhi Sultanate invasion and Pandya collapse (1310–1345 CE):**
The Pandya kingdom was devastated by the invasion of Malik Kafur, the general of the Delhi Sultanate under Alauddin Khalji, in 1310–11 CE. Malik Kafur sacked Madurai, plundered the Meenakshi temple, and carried off enormous treasure to Delhi — Ibn Battuta's account describes 512 elephants, 5,000 horses, and quantities of gold, silver, and precious stones. The Pandya princes fled and the kingdom fragmented. A successor state — the Madurai Sultanate — was established by the Khalji governor Ma'bar (1335–1378 CE).

## What were the pearl fisheries and their economic significance?

The Gulf of Mannar between the southern tip of India and northwestern Sri Lanka contained the most productive pearl oyster beds in the ancient world. The Pandya kingdom's control over these fisheries was a primary source of their wealth and international significance:

- **Pearl fishing methods:** Divers (typically from specific communities — Paravas on the Indian side, and Sinhalese on the Sri Lanka side) free-dived to depths of 20–30 meters, collecting oysters in nets. The diving season was typically in spring.
- **Roman demand:** Roman texts describe Indian pearls as the finest in the world — rounder, larger, and more lustrous than Persian Gulf pearls. Roman merchants paid enormous sums for large, perfect pearls.
- **The Paravas:** The pearl-fishing Parava community of the Gulf of Mannar converted en masse to Christianity in 1535–36 CE under the Portuguese missionary Francis Xavier, who baptized an estimated 10,000–30,000 Paravas. This mass conversion remains one of the most significant events in the history of Christianity in South Asia.

## What is the architectural and cultural legacy of the Pandyas?

**Madurai Meenakshi Amman Temple:**
The great temple complex at Madurai — dedicated to the goddess Meenakshi (a form of Parvati) and Sundareswarar (Shiva) — was substantially developed during the Pandya period, though the current structure dates mostly to the Nayak period (16th–17th centuries CE). The Pandyas built the original temple and the legendary Thousand-Pillar Hall (Ayiramkaal Mandapam). The temple complex covers approximately 45 acres and contains 14 gopurams (gateway towers), with the tallest rising to 51.9 meters.

**The Tirunelveli tradition:**
The Pandya heartland of Tirunelveli (Nellai) is famous for its own distinctive temple architecture (the Nellaiappar Temple), its literary tradition (the Tirunelveli Tamil has been described as the purest form of classical Tamil), and the Halwa of Tirunelveli — a cultural product with centuries of history.

**Jain heritage:**
The Pandya kingdom had a complex relationship with Jainism. Multiple Pandya kings converted to Jainism, and the kingdom was a significant center of Jain culture — evidenced by the rock-bed sculptures at Sittannavasal, Anaimalai, and other sites. The Shaiva Nayanmars — particularly Thirugnana Sambandar and Thirunavukkarasar — campaigned against Jain influence at the Pandya court, with hagiographies recording dramatic debates and conversions.
